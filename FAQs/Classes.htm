The issue of "Player Classes" brings to mind a question about suffering from skill-boredom. By this I mean, am I going to be standing in some building with a stick beating on a dummy for 2 weeks, before I can hunt?

No.  You can hunt immediately.  Remember (see "Characters" FAQs), when you first roll-up a character, you are going to play a short PRIVATE game.  If YOU want to mold your character from birth as a fighter, hunter, or whatever, then you can immediately seek conflict, because you will have years of specific training at your disposable.

Also note that repeated training is important to your future.  So, yes you can train with a Guild Master (or beat on a dummy) for a couple weeks to boost your skills further.  And, this will not be as boring and tedious as you might think, because your character can be doing other things at the same time.  As stated on the web page, "The Engine", your character will have "Independent and logical action:  Walk, talk, juggle, and think all concurrently".  Here is how to relieve the boredom...

Something new to MicroRealities games are things called, "Habits".  These allow you to automate many things your character does, like when training, practicing, teaching, or any other repetitive action.  This means in the case of training, you can carry on a relaxed conversation with someone else while going through your "forms", or if your habitual training requires less attention/intensity then read through your latest scroll, "Drowning: How to avoid it, using Magical Runes" at the same time.



In "Pravos", can I create (or pay for) special guild features, like a fountain that cures all diseases or an altar that enchants any wood item to a +1 stat/combat/etc.?

Yes.  You can either create it yourself "in game", or pay "out of game" for inner guild hall features.  However, the details of these "out of game" extras will not be discussed until Pravos enters public BETA.

Actually, your question has hit on one of the main differences between prayer and magic.  "In general", prayers invoke power from a higher source, and magic focuses your/local energies as the source.  So the praying character, at low skill level, would have a better chance of creating the fountain or altar you mentioned.  However, once a magic-user becomes highly skilled, they would know how to accomplish it just as easily.  This is not a "rule" about prayers and magic, only a hint at the difference - the source of the "real" power.

And remember (see "Stats" FAQs) that "+1" is a very subjective thing in a Vapors game.  In addition, keep in mind that it is you who defines a stat, combat add, defensive add, etc.  It is true that these things exist in a "raw physics form" and so when you create a perception for a stat, combat, and so on, you will be seeing a consistent and realistic representation of your perception.



I am thinking about creating some type of "Naturalist/Guardian" class, in the game "Pravos". Can I lay claim to a portion of undeveloped land and then create a guild hall out of the natural materials at hand?

Yes.  However, there is a distinction between 'undeveloped' and 'unclaimed' land.

For now, you must own the 'undeveloped' land before you may build a guild hall upon it.  This restriction might be loosened to a lease, which applies to any 'normal building', but that is doubtful.  In the case of 'unclaimed' land, the issue of staking/claiming new lands will not be worked out until Pravos enters BETA.  So, for now, consider all lands initially deeded to the nearest city and you must purchase, steal, or somehow receive ownership before construction should begin.

Your biggest hurdle will be to create a guild hall that will endure.  One that can weather the local environment, ensure that wandering creatures cannot destroy it, and so forth.  When you create a structure far from civilization, you will either know how to, or will hire someone else to, build and maintain your place.  This ensures that your guild truly represents you, for when future visitor's arrive.  In addition, you are probably going to invest a lot of resources on special inner-guild features that will allow your members unique boosts, training, etc., so you don't want to have a place that any old thief can get into, any contending guild can just puff and blow your walls down, and so on.

You might build your guild hall from the 'materials at hand' in a conventional sense - stone walls, wood roof, etc. - or use unconventional practices.  For instance, let's imagine that you wanted to "grow" your guild hall from a circle of trees.  Your imagination and your character's skill-set is the only limit - 'catwalks', guild rooms in the tree tops, magic sap for rejuvenation, and so on.  It will take time to grow all these things, even if you are a "master" magician (or whatever).  So you see, as long as the guild hall is recognizable as "your permanent" meeting place and has moderate protection, then a conventional or unconventional construction will be approved as a "guild hall".

One other issue that you should be considering.  If your guild hall ends up within the protection of a community (town, city, etc.), then you will probably be paying taxes to them for the land, protection, or whatever services they supply.  So, this means you should consider setting up some guild merchant NPCs (Non-Player Characters) to create, improve, or teach things to generate income for the guild.



How are dual, or multi, classes handled?

As long as you meet the requirements of each guild (class) then join as many as will accept you. (see the "How do I create a class?" question below)

However, if one of your guilds has a "charter" conflict with another guild, then you will not be allowed to join the new guild, until the conflict is resolved.  A charter defines and details itself through "line items", like the entrance exam, requirements to advance in rank, and requirements to hold an office.  Any line item can be an area of conflict.

In case you are wondering, it is not a simple matter to change a charter.  Each member to be considered as part of a class, must be approved by the guild.  This means that if a charter is changed, then you must resubmit yourself for acceptance.  If you have 'fickle' charter makers then take appropriate actions from within your guild.  From a "virtual reality environment" (VRE) point of view, your agreement is being nullified, since what you agreed to in the past is no longer true.  And from a "gaming" point of view, an inconsistency which could lead to unexpected "in game" consequences, possible cheating, and so on, is now avoided through the nullification approach.



How do I create a class?

When the game is released, you will create a new class using the "Class Constructor Screen".  You must have nine followers (players and/or NPCs) and a meeting place - a structure you own, a rented room, or any reserved area. You will have one year in which to buy or build your guild hall.

It is the "charter" which defines and details the rules of the class. Some charter fields require data, and some are optional (for role-playing or advanced play). Currently, the only required field is the entrance exam (the minimum levels of skill/experience/...).

An example of an advanced class called, "The Monks of Blue River", could be something like:

Entrance exam
Has a prayer skill level of 11+.
Has a gardening experience level of 5+.
Must have 1 "healing" and 1 "plant-related" prayer which is not known to the guild-class.


A "Monk of the Blue River" is one who
Never attacks, except when defending a plant.
Never turns away from healing an injured creature, unless the creature was seen injuring a plant.

(etc.)
